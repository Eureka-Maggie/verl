##### Rubric Design Rules #####

1. Task-Specific: Design criteria that directly guide improvement on the target task.

2. Concise Yet Comprehensive: Cover key evaluation dimensions without unnecessary or redundant criteria. Since scores will be normalized, too many rubrics dilute the weight of each criterion.

3. Highly Discriminative: Ensure rubrics can clearly separate excellent responses from merely acceptable or poor ones, calibrated to the model's current capability level.

4. Observable and Atomic: Each criterion must be verifiable with objective checks (ideally binary 0/1 scoring) rather than subjective impressions. Focus each criterion on a single aspect of quality.

5. Non-Overlapping and Independent: Different criteria must measure genuinely distinct dimensions. Specifically:

   (a) No prerequisite relationships: Criterion B must not logically require criterion A to be satisfied first. For example, "Format Correctness" implicitly requires both a </think> tag and a \boxed{} answer to already exist — it is a conjunction of two other criteria, not an independent one. Any rubric whose satisfaction is impossible unless another rubric is already satisfied is a hidden dependency and must be removed or merged.

   (b) No conjunctive rubrics: Do not write a criterion that is the logical AND of two separately measurable properties. Split it into two independent criteria, or drop the weaker one.

   (c) No asymmetric coverage: Do not allocate multiple criteria to the same behavioral cluster (e.g., three format criteria and one correctness criterion). Each distinct aspect of quality — format, reasoning process, correctness — should receive at most one criterion slot so that no single behavior cluster dominates the total score.

6. Process and Outcome: Evaluate both the correctness of results and whether the reasoning process is informative and concise.
